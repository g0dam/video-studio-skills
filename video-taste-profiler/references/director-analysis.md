# Director Analysis Framework

Use this guide to analyze video sources like a director, editor, cinematographer, and AI-video producer. The final taste should be specific enough to guide new work, but abstract enough to avoid copying a source.

## Analysis Layers

1. **Narrative engine**
   - What promise appears in the first seconds?
   - Is the story built around curiosity, confession, transformation, spectacle, texture, comedy, tension, or a reveal?
   - Where does the emotional or conceptual turn happen?
   - What does the ending leave behind: answer, image, joke, discomfort, aspiration, unresolved question?

2. **Shot grammar**
   - Dominant shot sizes: extreme wide, wide, medium, close-up, insert, POV, overhead, low angle.
   - Lens feel: compressed telephoto, phone-wide immediacy, macro texture, neutral documentary, stylized distortion.
   - Framing habits: centered symmetry, off-axis observation, foreground occlusion, reflection, doorway/window framing, negative space.
   - Blocking: how bodies enter, pause, cross, hide, reveal, touch objects, or face away.

3. **Camera motion**
   - Static hold, handheld drift, slow push, lateral track, whip pan, orbit, drone reveal, top-down move, locked-off tableau.
   - What motivates the move: subject action, emotional realization, location reveal, rhythm, object detail, transition?
   - AI risk: fast hands, complex crowds, reflective surfaces, rapid face turns, tiny text.

4. **Editing rhythm**
   - Average shot length and pattern: steady, bursty, slow-fast-slow, montage, one-take illusion.
   - Cut logic: match cut, action cut, sound bridge, object transition, jump cut, hard contrast, pause.
   - What is withheld and when the viewer is allowed to understand.

5. **Color, light, and texture**
   - Palette anchors, contrast, saturation, time of day, weather, practical light sources.
   - Material signatures: paper, glass, metal, water, fabric, skin, dust, neon, wood, concrete.
   - Grade behavior: clean commercial, film grain, low contrast, crushed blacks, soft highlights, phone-native.

6. **Sound and voice**
   - Music role: drive, atmosphere, irony, restraint, silence.
   - Natural sound: footsteps, paper, fabric, traffic, room tone, breath, mechanical hum.
   - Voice behavior: direct address, inner monologue, sparse line, punchline, no voice.

7. **Performance and presence**
   - Gesture scale: theatrical, restrained, micro-expression, documentary non-acting, comedic exaggeration.
   - Eye direction, pauses, reaction timing, body tension, interaction with props.
   - Whether the subject feels like a character, creator-host, observer, product user, or symbolic body.

8. **Platform grammar**
   - First-frame readability, hook density, caption behavior, aspect ratio, loopability, subtitle dependence.
   - Whether the piece expects sound on or sound off.
   - Thumbnail/cover moments and title logic.

## Taste Translation

Translate observations into reusable rules:

- From "this creator always uses coffee shops" to "intimate third-place interiors with reflective surfaces and small hand actions."
- From "same joke format" to "setup in ordinary situation, visual contradiction at midpoint, one understated reaction shot as punchline."
- From "looks like Wong Kar-wai" to "compressed night interiors, saturated practical light, reflection layers, longing expressed through blocked movement."

## Confidence Labels

Use confidence labels in the taste:

- `high`: repeated across several sources or strongly visible in the provided material.
- `medium`: plausible pattern with limited evidence.
- `low`: single-source inference, unclear image quality, or user-provided description without direct source.

## Red Lines

- Do not promise exact replication of a living creator's identity, face, voice, catchphrases, private life, or brand.
- Do not copy full scripts, transcripts, protected character designs, logos, or scene-by-scene structures.
- Do not treat one viral video as the entire taste unless the user explicitly asks for a single-video analysis; mark it as low coverage.

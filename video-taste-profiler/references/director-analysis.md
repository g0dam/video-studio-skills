# Director Analysis Framework

Use this guide to analyze video sources like a director, writer, cinematographer, production designer, editor, and sound designer. The final taste should be specific enough to guide new work, but abstract enough to avoid copying a source.

The goal is not to describe "what appeared in this video." The goal is to infer the source's repeatable taste grammar: how story, space, bodies, camera, light, color, edit, transition, and sound cooperate to create the viewing experience.

## Operating Rules

- Cover every layer below when evidence allows. If a layer is not visible or audible, mark it low confidence instead of guessing.
- Separate observed evidence from inference. Name the source IDs or local frames that support repeated patterns.
- Translate concrete source details into reusable craft rules. Do not preserve copyrighted characters, full scripts, exact layouts, logos, faces, voices, props, costumes, or scene-by-scene structures.
- Prefer production language that can drive later scripts, shotboards, image generation, and render prompts.
- Put concrete source inventory in `source-log.md`. Put the abstracted director grammar in `taste.md`.
- For every dimension, separate three levels: observed evidence, inferred pattern, and reusable rule. The final taste should emphasize the reusable rule.
- Avoid one-off nouns as style definitions. A "gift box" is not a taste; "object-triggered reveal that turns ordinary space into fantasy space" can be a taste rule.

## Dimension Extraction Contract

For each director dimension, capture the work at four levels:

- **Observed evidence**: neutral facts with source IDs, frame/time references, or audio notes. Keep this in `source-log.md`.
- **Inferred pattern**: the craft mechanism suggested by the evidence, such as delayed information, low-angle intimacy, warm/cool emotional contrast, or sound-led transition.
- **Reusable rule**: a source-agnostic instruction that can guide a new original story, shotboard, render prompt, or edit.
- **Copy-risk redline**: the exact things that must not transfer, such as protected characters, names, plot sequence, signature props, costumes, logos, readable text, voice, music, dialogue, or distinctive set layout.

If a dimension cannot produce a reusable rule without keeping source-specific nouns, keep it as evidence only. Strong taste profiles are specific about craft and abstract about source identity.

## Abstraction Ladder

Use this ladder when converting source material into taste:

1. Literal inventory: "a specific character uses a specific object in a specific scene."
2. Dramatic function: "an ordinary object carries hidden emotional information."
3. Viewer effect: "the audience reinterprets a familiar behavior and feels comic recognition."
4. Reusable production rule: "use a newly invented everyday object as a meaning bridge between practical reality and inner emotional logic."
5. Boundary: "do not reuse the original object, character design, dialogue, shot order, setting layout, or ending image."

Only levels 2-5 should influence `taste.md`; level 1 belongs in `source-log.md`.

## Comprehensive Analysis Layers

1. **Narrative and structure**
   - Theme, core conflict, story goal, character desire, character arc, three-act shape, setup-build-turn-payoff, suspense, foreshadowing, reversal, information order, point of view, narration, timeline, flashback, flashforward, ellipsis, repetition, parallel storytelling, beat points, climax, and ending aftertaste.
   - Ask: what promise appears in the first seconds, what is withheld, when does the viewer understand, and what remains after the ending?

2. **Scene and world**
   - Era, location, social class, space scale, environment function, weather, season, geography, realism, stylization, cultural symbols, prop system, set density, movement path, spatial pressure, and spatial openness.
   - Ask: does the world feel lived-in, symbolic, theatrical, documentary, commercial, game-like, or dream-like?

3. **Mise-en-scene**
   - Everything in front of the camera: set, props, light, costume, makeup, actor position, action, and composition.
   - Track staging, foreground/midground/background relations, prop placement, visual center, occlusion, depth, symmetry/asymmetry, crowdedness/emptiness, realism/theatricality, object metaphor, environmental storytelling, in-frame movement, and off-screen space.

4. **Character and performance**
   - Identity, age impression, occupation signal, psychological state, emotional intensity, face, eyes, posture, gait, gesture, breath, pause, speed, tone, dialogue subtext, distance, power relation, intimacy, conflict level, group blocking, improvisational feel, restraint, and exaggeration.
   - Ask: does performance carry meaning through text, micro-action, silence, reaction, or spectacle?

5. **Shot size**
   - Extreme wide, wide, full shot, medium full, medium, medium close-up, close-up, extreme close-up, insert, over-the-shoulder, POV, reaction shot, establishing shot, empty shot, and detail shot.
   - Ask: which distances are repeated, and what emotional access or distance do they create?

6. **Camera angle and position**
   - Eye level, high angle, low angle, bird's-eye, worm's-eye, front, side, rear, three-quarter, subjective, objective, surveillance, voyeur, ground-level, shoulder-behind, mirror position, and obstructed position.
   - Ask: does the camera grant power, reduce power, spy, confess, observe, or trap?

7. **Camera movement**
   - Locked-off, push, pull, pan, tilt, lateral move, boom, follow, orbit, handheld, steadicam, dolly, crane, aerial, whip pan, zoom, roll, rack focus, dolly zoom, subjective shake, breathing motion, and mechanical motion.
   - Ask: what motivates movement: action, emotion, reveal, rhythm, object detail, transition, or platform energy?

8. **Composition and visual design**
   - Aspect ratio, subject placement, rule of thirds, centered framing, symmetry, frame-within-frame, leading lines, negative space, headroom, look room, depth layers, foreground occlusion, repeated patterns, shape language, circular/triangular/square composition, visual tension, balance, pressure, isolation, and group feeling.
   - Ask: how does the frame organize attention before the edit moves on?

9. **Optics and image texture**
   - Focal length, wide/normal/telephoto feel, distortion, compression, depth of field, shallow focus, deep focus, focus pull, exposure, shutter feel, motion blur, grain, sharpness, glare, lens flare, filters, haze, soft focus, lens dirt, realism, advertising polish, documentary texture, film feel, and digital feel.
   - Ask: what optical choices make the world intimate, monumental, unstable, tactile, synthetic, or real?

10. **Lighting**
    - Source direction, key, fill, rim, eye light, top light, underlight, side light, backlight, natural light, hard light, soft light, high key, low key, contrast ratio, shadow shape, light spots, window light, neon, candlelight, flicker, moving light, color temperature, warm/cool contrast, and lighting motivation.
    - Ask: where does the light come from inside the world, and where is it purely expressive?

11. **Color and art direction**
    - Main color, support color, accent color, warm/cool balance, saturation, brightness, contrast, skin tone, color emotion, color symbolism, character color, location color, color shift, black-and-white, monochrome, complementary color, analogous color, retro color, commercial color, documentary color, and dream color.
    - Ask: which colors carry story, identity, class, time, mood, or platform readability?

12. **Editing and rhythm**
    - Shot length, average shot duration, cut frequency, fast cutting, long take, rhythmic escalation, rhythmic interruption, action match, eye-line match, screen direction, axis, jump cut, cross-cutting, parallel cutting, montage, intercutting, ellipsis, repetition, pause, reaction shot, acceleration, and release.
    - Ask: how does editing construct space, time, attention, emotion, and payoff?

13. **Transitions and graphic links**
    - Hard cut, fade in, fade out, dissolve, wipe, occlusion transition, match cut, graphic match, action match, sound lead-in, sound continuation, white flash, black flash, glitch transition, whip transition, object wipe, time-lapse transition, spatial jump, and emotional transition.
    - Ask: are transitions invisible continuity, graphic pleasure, emotional punctuation, or platform spectacle?

14. **Sound and music**
    - Dialogue, ambience, foley, sound effects, music, silence, sync, counterpoint, diegetic sound, non-diegetic sound, subjective sound, objective sound, sound bridge, narration, motif music, theme, beat, tempo, volume, pitch, timbre, reverb, spatiality, low-frequency impact, emotional setup, music entry/exit, and synchronization with edit points.
    - Ask: what would be lost if the video were muted, and what must still read if it is watched sound-off?

15. **Platform and AI production behavior**
    - First-frame readability, hook density, safe areas, caption behavior, cover/thumbnail moment, loopability, sound-on versus sound-off assumptions, renderability, model failure risks, continuity locks, reusable prompt vocabulary, and where image generation can reduce drift.
    - Ask: what should downstream story, asset, shotboard, and render skills preserve?

## Taste Translation

Translate observations into reusable rules:

- From "this creator always uses coffee shops" to "intimate third-place interiors with reflective surfaces and small hand actions."
- From "same joke format" to "setup in ordinary situation, visual contradiction at midpoint, one understated reaction shot as punchline."
- From "looks like Wong Kar-wai" to "compressed night interiors, saturated practical light, reflection layers, longing expressed through blocked movement."
- From "lots of close-ups" to "emotion is revealed through inserts and reaction close-ups before full spatial context."
- From "quiet sad dialogue" to "subtext carried by pauses, breath, object handling, and late sound bridge rather than explanatory lines."
- From "the video uses a cat, a gift, and a pool" to "a familiar domestic setup is interrupted by a tactile object reveal, then briefly opens into a heightened fantasy space before returning to reaction comedy."
- From "this imitates a famous animal animation film" to "family-animation polish with species-aware performance, readable silhouettes, tactile material detail, and optimistic world logic."

For each translation, also write the substitution rule: what a new work should invent instead. Example: "Use a newly designed companion, household object, room layout, and emotional reveal while preserving the function of companion-level observation, tactile detail insert, and warm reaction payoff."

## Output Discipline

In `taste.md`, each major section should prefer this shape:

```text
- Observed evidence summary:
- Abstract pattern:
- Viewer effect:
- Reusable production rule:
- Copy-risk boundary:
- Evidence confidence:
```

This does not mean every bullet must use these labels, but the reasoning must be present. Avoid a section that only names objects, characters, locations, or plot events from the source.

The 14 director dimensions are mandatory when evidence allows:

1. Narrative and structure.
2. Scene and world.
3. Mise-en-scene.
4. Character and performance.
5. Shot size.
6. Camera angle and position.
7. Camera movement.
8. Composition and visual design.
9. Optics and image texture.
10. Lighting.
11. Color and art direction.
12. Editing and rhythm.
13. Transitions and graphic links.
14. Sound and music.

Platform and AI production behavior is an additional downstream layer, not a substitute for the director analysis.

## Confidence Labels

Use confidence labels in the taste:

- `high`: repeated across several sources or strongly visible in the provided material.
- `medium`: plausible pattern with limited evidence.
- `low`: single-source inference, unclear image quality, unavailable audio, missing motion evidence, or user-provided description without direct source.

## Red Lines

- Do not promise exact replication of a living creator's identity, face, voice, catchphrases, private life, or brand.
- Do not copy full scripts, transcripts, protected character designs, logos, or scene-by-scene structures.
- Do not treat one viral video as the entire taste unless the user explicitly asks for a single-video analysis; mark it as low coverage.
- Do not preserve the source's unique bundle of character + setting + prop + conflict + shot order. Even if each element is generic alone, the bundle can still read as copying.
- Do not make negative constraints vague. Name the source-specific elements that must be replaced, then name the abstract function that should remain.

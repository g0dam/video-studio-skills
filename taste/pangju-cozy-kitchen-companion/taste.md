# Pangju Cozy Kitchen Companion

## Essence

This taste turns a fixed chubby orange cat protagonist into short, repeatable social micro-dramas inside a classic family animated feature world: rounded storybook sets, golden practical light, expressive face acting, soft color contrast, and tactile hand-crafted props. A tiny companion performs a human-scale care ritual, stumbles through the practical steps with warm physical comedy, then reveals that the effort was for someone else. The craft is not "cute cat posing"; it is small-body procedure plus emotional usefulness.

## Best For

- 20-35 second vertical AI pet micro-dramas.
- Serialized episodes about cooking, working, delivering food, running a stall, or helping neighbors.
- Premium storybook animated shorts with classic family-film warmth and social-platform hooks.
- Stories where a physical process can be shown clearly: carrying, stirring, pushing, hiding, sharing, serving.

## Narrative and Structure

- Hook: open on Pangju doing an impossible-but-readable human task in the first second: carrying a basket, stirring noodles, guarding a stall, delivering a bowl, counting coins.
- Build: show 2-3 concrete procedural steps with one small failure caused by cat anatomy or round body weight.
- Turn: reveal that the task is for a human or smaller vulnerable figure, not for Pangju's own appetite.
- Payoff: end on a small action, not a speech: a bowl accepted, a light turning on, a hand softening, Pangju hiding behind the door.
- Common motifs: hot food, rain, old corridors, market mornings, cheap kitchen tools, small coins, waiting lights, low tables, worn cloth.
- Information reveal order: abnormal behavior first, motive later, emotion last.
- Ending aftertaste: warm and slightly funny, leaving viewers to choose Pangju's next human task.

## Scene and World

- Era and location: contemporary Chinese life translated into a stylized storybook neighborhood, not photoreal old streets, fantasy kingdoms, or animal cities.
- Social class and cultural signals: modest homes, small shops, practical objects, ordinary neighbors, no luxury-brand framing.
- Space scale and geography: places must be navigable from cat height: floor, stool, low table, door gap, basket, counter edge.
- Weather and season: rain, dawn, night-market humidity, kitchen steam, and cool corridor air work well as emotional contrast.
- Realism versus stylization: strongly stylized classic family animation taste; rounded architecture, softened props, expressive proportions, painterly surfaces, emotionally believable but not photoreal.
- Prop system and set density: few props, high tactility; one hero prop per beat.
- Movement path and spatial pressure: Pangju crosses thresholds: room to corridor, market to kitchen, kitchen to human room, stall to street.

## Mise-en-scene

- Staging: keep Pangju, the task object, and the human consequence readable in vertical frame.
- Foreground/midground/background: use doorframes, table edges, steam, hanging cloth, and basket rims to create intimacy.
- Prop placement and visual center: props sit low and close to paws; bowls and spoons are scaled for a cat to push or hold.
- Occlusion and depth: doorway peeks and counter edges make Pangju feel small without hiding the action.
- Symmetry/asymmetry: round Pangju in the center for hooks; asymmetrical side peeks for payoffs.
- Object metaphor: food and tools carry care, not plot exposition.
- Environmental storytelling: soft flour marks, old enamel, honey-colored wood, rounded doorframes, cozy lamp glow, and handcrafted wear imply humble daily life without becoming gritty realism.

## Character and Performance

- Role identity and status: Pangju is not a mascot mascot-pose character; it is a tiny worker trying to be useful.
- Emotional temperature: sincere, soft, lightly comic, never cynical or humiliating.
- Face and eye behavior: large amber eyes lead comprehension; eyes widen during mistakes and soften during payoff.
- Posture, gait, gesture, breath: low center of gravity, short-legged shuffle, two-paw careful holding, tail follow-through, ear flattening when startled.
- Dialogue and subtext: Pangju never speaks. Human dialogue is sparse and added in post only if needed.
- Distance, power, and intimacy: humans should lower toward Pangju's level at recognition, but not fully understand everything too early.
- Restraint versus exaggeration: exaggerate timing and expression, not anatomy. No human hands, white gloves, human teeth, or long upright walking.

## Shot Size

- Dominant shot distances: close and medium-close, because face acting and objects carry the story.
- Inserts and details: paws on bowl, spoon in pot, flour on nose, basket handle, steam, apron stitch, small coin pouch.
- Reaction shots: Pangju reaction first, human reaction second, then final shared frame or doorway composition.
- Establishing or empty shots: avoid generic beauty establishing shots; every opening frame must contain action.

## Camera Angle and Position

- Angle habits: cat-height, tabletop edge, floor-level tracking, small upward angle toward oversized storybook human spaces.
- Subjective/objective viewpoint: mostly objective but close to Pangju's physical perspective.
- Obstructed, mirror, surveillance, or voyeur positions: door gaps, shelf gaps, basket rim, curtain edge; surveillance style only if clearly framed as AI fiction.
- Power relation created by camera height: low camera makes human spaces large and Pangju's effort heroic without making the world epic.

## Camera Movement

- Movement types: gentle push-in, tiny tracking follow, motivated tilt to prop, small handheld-like breathing only if it supports living texture.
- Movement motivation: follow Pangju's attention or the object being carried.
- Handheld/mechanical/breathing feel: stable premium animation with slight organic drift, not chaotic vlog shake.
- Focus movement: rack or attention shift from prop to eyes, then from bowl/object to human reaction.

## Composition and Visual Design

- Aspect ratio and safe area: 9:16; keep head, paws, bowl, and caption area clear.
- Subject placement: Pangju can fill the center for hooks; emotional reveals work with Pangju foreground and human background.
- Symmetry, negative space, leading lines: corridors, counters, and table edges guide the eye to the small action.
- Depth layers and foreground: use foreground objects to frame scale, not clutter.
- Shape language and visual tension: Pangju's round silhouette contrasts with thin tools, tall doorframes, and narrow stools.

## Optics and Image Texture

- Focal length feel: medium lens feel, slight closeness, no extreme distortion.
- Depth of field: shallow enough for intimacy, but keep task objects readable.
- Exposure and shutter feel: clean animated exposure; no harsh strobe or action blur.
- Grain, sharpness, haze, flare, dirt: soft haze from steam or rain is acceptable; avoid dirty realism, documentary grime, and live-action texture.
- Film/digital/documentary/commercial feel: classic family animated feature with tactile food-commercial inserts, not live-action realism.

## Lighting

- Motivated sources: golden kitchen bulb, lavender-blue dawn spill, rounded hallway glow, rainy street reflections, cozy shop warmer.
- Key/fill/rim/eye light: warm key on Pangju's face and eyes, cool fill from corridor or morning exterior.
- Hard/soft, high-key/low-key: soft and readable; low-key only in the first hook, never too dark.
- Shadow shape: soft contact shadows under paws and bowl.
- Color temperature and moving light: warm interior versus cool exterior can mark the emotional turn.

## Color and Art Direction

- Main/support/accent colors: warm orange cat, cream apron, muted gray old street, wood brown, tiny blue enamel rim.
- Warm/cool, saturation, brightness, contrast: warm subject against restrained cool environment; avoid one-color orange dominance.
- Skin tone behavior: human skin stays natural and softly lit.
- Color symbolism: blue enamel and lavender dawn signal the outside world; warm soup/kitchen light signals care.
- Character/location color rules: Pangju remains the warmest shape; locations stay humble and text-free.
- Color shift across the piece: cooler mystery start, warmer payoff end.

## Editing and Rhythm

- Shot length: 3-8 second scenes; micro-beats inside each clip should be handled by action.
- Cut frequency: fast enough for platform retention, slow enough for tactile readability.
- Match logic and continuity: cut by object continuity: basket to ingredient, ingredient to pot, spoon to bowl, bowl to human.
- Montage, jump cut, cross-cutting, repetition: use compact procedure montage, not random cute cutaways.
- Pause, acceleration, release: brief acceleration during the mistake, then a held recognition beat at the end.

## Transitions and Graphic Links

- Transition types: hard cuts and attention cuts are enough.
- Graphic or action matches: round bowl to round face, spoon stir to tail sweep, door gap to eye peek.
- Sound lead-in or continuation: use foley bridges: spoon clink, rain, simmer, door creak.
- Time/space/emotion transition logic: object travels across spaces and carries the emotional meaning.

## Sound and Music

- Dialogue: sparse human lines only in post; Pangju never speaks.
- Ambience: dawn market, hallway hum, kitchen simmer, rain, room tone.
- Foley and effects: paw steps, basket rustle, bowl scrape, spoon clink, flour puff, steam.
- Music role and motif: soft plucks or piano enter lightly, with a warmer cue at the reveal.
- Silence: hold near-silence before human recognition.
- Diegetic/non-diegetic behavior: foley should feel diegetic; music should stay low.
- Sound bridge: prop foley can connect cuts.
- Mix, reverb, spatiality: small rooms should sound close and dry; rainy alleys can have soft exterior reverb.

## Platform and AI Production Behavior

- First-frame rule: show abnormal action, not a generic cute face.
- Caption/subtitle behavior: all captions, AI labels, time stamps, and comment hooks are post-production overlays.
- Cover or thumbnail moment: Pangju holding a spoon or pushing a hot bowl through a doorway.
- Loop behavior: final comment question should point to the next task: work, open stall, deliver food, save a neighbor.
- Sound-on/sound-off assumptions: visual story must work silently; sound improves warmth.
- Render risks: over-humanized cat, chef hat drift, fake readable market text, prop morphing, kitchen fire danger, human hand drift, excessive brand signage.
- Image-generation opportunities: recurring locations, props, granny, market stall, noodle kitchen, rain doorway, and episode cover.
